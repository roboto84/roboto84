Why I Develop Software #

Software development may seem like a novel endeavor when taking into account the variety of professions throughout human civilization, but in reality software development has roots in one of the oldest trades: storytelling. From telling the story of us through history, and the story of our resource management with accounting, software development is just the latest iteration of storytelling, by telling the story of data and what it means to us as a people.

My Software Philosophy #

When it comes to creating software, my general sense is that it is much more of an art form akin to traditional writing more than anything else. Developers, programmers, and software engineers all use math in the form of Boolean logic (along with well known patterns) to produce results, but it is hard to ignore the similarities between a writer's creative/editing process and the similar process that occurs when creating programs at different scales. I try to think about that when I am creating software. I keep the fundamentals in mind of course, but also the following:

  • What story is my software trying to tell? Is the problem my software is trying to help solve well-defined?
  • Is my software story worth telling? Am I re-inventing the wheel? Does some lib already do it?
  • Am I telling my software story in a concise and efficient way? (design patterns, language features)
  • Can my software story reach my audience? (UI, UX, scaling considerations, platform choice, data management, network management)
  • Have I made the most of my software storytelling tools? (language features, editor automation, software libraries)

How I Approach Software Problems #

Most problems in software are actually a collection of smaller problems that need to be solved. Nowadays, software developers have the privilege of leveraging decades worth of industry problem-solving. This means that a subset of problems I want to solve may already be solved, or have a large amount of literature on different approaches to solving them. Doing some research will likely solve 95% of my problems easily and quickly, saving my energy for more novel issues.

"There is only one good, knowledge, and one evil, ignorance." – Socrates

When I have a neoteric problem, it's important to do what I can to truly understand it before attempting to solve it. This saves time and resources in the long run. The problem can be probed with the following initial questions:

  • Does the problem really need to be solved, or is my energy better spent elsewhere?
  • If it does need to be solved, how much time and resources do I have available to solve it?
  • What does the data domain look like?
  • What does the literature say about this type of data, if any?

Once the problem is probed, I can design a solution that focuses on efficiency, creativity, and flexibility. It is important to understand and accept that a solution can be impacted by the amount of time and resources allotted for it. Fortunately, I believe that software problem-solving can be an iterative process in which "finished is better than perfect" and perfection can be achieved given enough iterations. The iteration investment is a decision that would have to be made and balanced with other concerns.
