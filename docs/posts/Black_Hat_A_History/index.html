Black Hat Origins

26 Nov 2023 • 7 MIN READ

Introduction #

The world of Cybersecurity is enthralled in the story of an eternal cat and mouse game between parties interested in protecting resources and parties interested in acquiring those resources. Some days teams play a defensive role protecting resources, while other days those same teams may be more interested in playing an offensive role. In some ways this story parallels the state of how the natural world works. The term hacker has been endowed to both sides of this defense/offense game, two sides of the same coin. What is the history of this term that has become so prominent in popular culture?

Origins #

The world’s earliest origination of the term hacker was back in the 13th century from an old high German word meaning the act of chopping or hacking away at an object. To whither it down to its more fundamental parts. From this idea of breaking things down to a more fundamental component level, the term hacking was used figuratively to denote the act of crudely or ruthlessly working on something or simply toiling with it. In the context of computers, the term hack was first used by the MIT Tech Model Railroad Club in 1955 to describe working on the electrical system of the club’s model train set requesting that,

"...anyone working or hacking on the electrical system turn the power off to avoid fuse blowing." 1

The Jargon File, which was created in 1975, was a glossary and usage dictionary of slang used by computer programmers. It’s definition for the term hacker is,

"A person who enjoys exploring the details of programmable systems and how to stretch their capabilities, as opposed to most users, who prefer to learn only the minimum necessary." 2 3

It also makes sure it includes the less desired definition of the term hacker and depreciates it by stating,

"[deprecated] A malicious meddler who tries to discover sensitive information by poking around. Hence password hacker, network hacker. The correct term for this sense is cracker." 2 3

By the 1980s the term also came to describe individuals who could create computer code that might not be of the highest standards, but could still get the job done even if it involved ignoring, or breaching an organization’s computer security. In today’s vernacular the term describes people that can workaround computer security systems and come up with innovative ways to make computer systems work in an originally unintended way. An individual with such a skill set that uses it for malicious intent is commonly classified as a Black Hat hacker, which would be classified by The Jargon File as a cracker.


The first published use of the term hacker (11/20/1963)

Unfortunately over time the term hacker was corrupted by computer laypeople to mean black hat hacker. In 1984 Steven Levy wrote a history of below-the-radar programmers and innovators in a book titled Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution. In the 1993 edition he lamented that,

"[the] word hacker had acquired a specific and negative connotation. The trouble began with some well-publicized arrests of teenagers who electronically ventured into forbidden digital grounds, like government computer systems. It was understandable that the journalists covering these stories would refer to the young perps as hackers. After all, that’s what the kids called themselves." 4

No one knows who the first individual that could be classified as a black hat hacker was. Alas, that fact may be lost to history, but the sense of black-hat activity could be first attributed to MIT hackers who were,

"tying up all the tie-lines between Harvard and M.I.T., or making long-distance calls by charging them to a local radar installation." 5 6

Phreaking #

This hints that initial black hat hackers first started in compromising telephone line networks to make free long distance phone calls, also culturally known in the hacker community as Phreaking. If original black hats can be traced to the activities of telephone phreaking, then perhaps Joe Engressia (Joy Bubbles), a blind seven-year-old boy, could be considered the first black hat due to his discovery that whistling at a frequency of 2637.02 Hz allowed him to exploit the phone networks to make free phone calls 7. Or perhaps it was his friend John Draper who dawned the hacker handle Captain Crunch for discovering and exploiting the fact that a free whistle being given out in Cap’n Crunch cereal boxes also produced a 2600 Hz tone when blown 7.


First cover of popular hacker magazine "2600"

Forerunners #

Some notable black hat hackers were able to pull off audacious feats even in the early days. Kevin Poulsen, also known as Dark Dante, was able to take over all the telephone lines for Los Angeles radio station KIIS-FM, assuring that he would be the 102nd caller and winning the prize of a Porsche 944 sports car in 1990. When Mr. Poulsen was featured on the NBC show Unsolved Mysteries, the show’s telephone lines mysteriously crashed 9. He was finally arrested in 1991 and served a 5 year sentence in federal prison. Another notable black hat hacker was Robert Morris who is known for creating the Morris worm in 1988 which is considered to be the first computer worm on the Internet. The worm exploited several vulnerabilities in the Unix sendmail program, a buffer overflow in the finger network service, and a lack of passwords configured for the remote execution on Remote Shell program at the time. Mr. Morris was sentenced to three year’s probation, and fined $10,000 10. Finally, Albert Gonzalez is known for masterminding the largest credit car theft and reselling over 170 million credit card, and ARM numbers from 2005 through 2007. His team used SQL injection to create backdoors into various corporate systems and then launch packet-sniffing attacks to steal credit card data from internal corporate networks 11. He was sentence to 20 years in federal prison in 2010. He was released September 19, 2023.
